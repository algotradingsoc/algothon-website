About

Algothon Remote in partnership with Aspect is the third annual Algothon organised by the Imperial College Algorithmic Trading Society. We’re excited to bring you 48 hours of intense hacking, bringing 150 of the brightest minds together to produce incredible new financial technology.

We’re delighted to offer several talks from field leading researchers in machine learning, as well as workshops to get you up to speed on some of the most exciting new technologies and practices in the field.

This will be an incredible weekend, producing amazing new technologies, learning from some of the best minds in the field, and overall having a great time!

Organisers

The Algorithmic Trading Society was founded with the aim of bringing students from all background together to develop their understanding of the application algorithms in a financially driven context. We aim to narrow the gap between university studies and the professional knowledge required in the field. Our events are tailored for our members, from learning basic concepts, to advanced trading simulations. Our members gain exclusive exposure to our sponsors through workshops and competitions. Whether you’re a novice to trading or a veteran Quant we welcome all to be part of the society.

Dr Rui Carvalho

Dr Rui Carvalho

Assistant Professor
Department of Engineering, Durham University

Extracting Governing Equations from Data

Abstract: Over the centuries, scientists have sought to describe natural phenomena with mechanistic models. In the last century, we have also made tremendous progress in building powerful non-mechanistic models, from linear regression to random forests and neural networks. However, although data are nowadays abundant in many areas of science and engineering, models often remain elusive. I will introduce the toolbox of sparse regression and illustrate how it can extract parsimonious models from data. I will give the examples of charging electric vehicles and automating the discovery of governing equations from data. I will also discuss how to use statistical tools to ensure that the extracted mathematical models are reliable.

Bio: Rui Carvalho is an Assistant Professor in the Department of Engineering, Durham University. His research focuses on the integration of methods of convex optimization and statistical machine learning. He joined Durham in 2015 from the Statistical Laboratory, University of Cambridge, where he was a Senior Research Associate. He obtained a PhD in Theoretical Physics from Instituto Superior Técnico, Lisbon, Portugal in 2001. He has been a Researcher Co-Investigator on EPSRC grants totalling over £1.1M, is a member of the EPSRC Peer Review College, and has acted as a panel member in four EPSRC calls. Rui Carvalho’s research has been reviewed in Nature News, MIT Technology Review and [Phys.org](http://phys.org/).

Prof Anil Bharath

Prof Anil Bharath

Fellow of the Institute of Engineering and Technology
Imperial College London

Fireside Chat: Explainable Machine Learning

Bio: Anil Anthony Bharath (Fellow of the Institute of Engineering & Technology) is Professor of Biologically-Inspired Computation & Inference at Imperial College. He holds a first degree from UCL (Elec Eng) and a PhD from Imperial College London. From 2011-2014, he was Associate Director of the Imperial Institute for Security Science & Technology. He subsequently joined the research board of the Data Sciences Institute (2014-2018). Motivated by discovering parallels between biological and machine intelligence, his current research interests are around architectures of convolutional neural networks; his lab has recently published on generative adversarial networks and deep reinforcement learning for visuo-motor control. He is academic founder of Cortexica Vision Systems, a company that applies parallel computing for fast indexing and retrieval of visual data for commercial applications of "visual search” – the ability to query very large databases based on visual appearance. Cortexica was the first company to provide commercial visual search services using cloud-based GPU architectures. Anil’s research group (Biologically-Inspired Computation and Inference) in the Department of Bioengineering works on biologically-inspired algorithm development, including artificial neural networks. Anil and his team have authored or co-authored papers in the areas of deep learning and computer vision, and have worked on the applications of these techniques to a wide variety of domains, including medical, non-medical and microscopy. He has acted as Associate Editor for Neural Networks, and as reviewer for Pattern Recognition, IEEE Transactions on Neural Networks and IEEE Transactions on Image Processing. With the late Maria Petrou, he co-edited Next Generation Artificial Vision Systems: Reverse Engineering the Human Visual System.

Heishiro Kanagawa

Heishiro Kanagawa

PhD Student in Machine Learning
Gatsby Computational Neuroscience Unit, UCL

An Interpretable Test of Model Comparison

Abstract: Recent advances in machine learning have provided effective generative models for very complex and high-dimensional data. A question of practical interest is how we should choose an appropriate model from a set of competing models. In this talk, I will present a solution to this problem: an interpretable test of model comparison. Given two models, our test enables us to check what one model is better than the other in terms of the fit to data; e.g., in image generation, what kind of images is a model better at generating than the other?
Bio: Heishiro Kanagawa is a graduate student pursuing a PhD in machine learning at Gatsby Computational Neuroscience Unit, University College London. He received BSc and MSc from Tokyo Institute of Technology. His research focus has been on the evaluation of complex generative models.

Judging

We have decided to change the style of Algothon this year, and it is going to be more suited for the remote environment. We all know how hard it is to get started with the broad themes we have had in previous years, so we have done a bit of tweaking to the model. Instead of having one big challenge where you are not given any description of the expected outcome of your work, we have designed a series of smaller challenges where you will get to demonstrate your skills and your speed at solving complex problems involving data analysis, deep learning, and all the usual stuff. These challenges will take you some time to complete and will all be released at the beginning of the event. There will be extra points for the first 3, 5 and 10 teams to solve each challenge with an accurate or correct solution. There will be a fixed number of points for each challenge solved regardless of your speed, and at the end of the event the teams with most points will win the prizes. The judging will thereby be mostly objective, comparing the quality and accuracy of your solutions against our benchmarks. It will make it easier for everyone involved, and you won’t have to go through tens of virtual judging rounds.

Code of Conduct

We believe that every single person has the right to participate and hack in a safe and welcoming environment.

Harassment includes but is not limited to offensive verbal or written comments related to gender, age, sexual orientation, disability, physical appearance, body size, race, religion, social class, economic status, veteran status, sexual images, deliberate intimidation, stalking, following, harassing photography or recording, sustained disruption of talks or other events, inappropriate physical contact, and unwelcome sexual attention. If what you’re doing is making someone feel uncomfortable, that counts as harassment and is enough reason to stop doing it.

Any person(s) involved in or present at the event asked to stop any harassing behaviour are expected to comply immediately.

Judges, mentors, volunteers, organizers, staff, and anyone else involved in or present at the event are also subject to the anti-harassment policy. In particular, attendees should not use sexualised images, activities, or other material both in their hacks and during the event. Staff (including volunteers) should not use sexualised clothing/uniforms/costumes, or otherwise create a sexualised environment.

If a participant engages in harassing behaviour, the organizers may take any action it deems appropriate, including warning the offender or expulsion from the event with no eligibility for reimbursement or refund of any type.

If you are being harassed, notice that someone else is being harassed, or have any other concerns, please contact a member of the organizing committee present at the event either in person or through email to the committee.

The organizing committee will be happy to help participants contact campus security or local law enforcement, provide escorts, or otherwise assist those experiencing harassment to feel safe for the duration of the event. We value your attendance.

We expect participants to follow these rules at the event venue, online interactions in relation to the event, event-related social events, and on supplied transportation if any.

Reporting Procedures

If you feel uncomfortable or think there may be a potential violation of the code of conduct, please report it immediately to a member of the organizing committee present at the event or through email to the organizing committee at algo.trade@imperial.ac.uk. All reporters have the right to remain anonymous.

Terms and Conditions

The organizers reserve the right to revise, make exceptions to, or otherwise amend these policies in whole or in part. If you have any questions regarding the code of conduct for this event, please contact the organizing committee by email at algo.trade@imperial.ac.uk.
